The Semester, Module by Module

What I accomplished each week

Six weeks of goal setting, self-assessment, and career preparation. Below is the purpose of each assignment, a representative sample, and the insight I extracted from it.

Week 1

Knowing Myself

Purpose: Establish the tone for the entire course — articulate why I'm pursuing this degree before undertaking anything else, and obtain an external assessment of my personality type in a professional context.
From the Career Goal Essay "I do not simply want to work in technology; I want to lead within it, shaping how organizations design, implement, and optimize the systems that drive their performance."
From the ESFP-T Personality Report My genuine strength isn't merely interpersonal aptitude — it's that I cultivate positive environments deliberately, a tendency apparently substantiated by actual research rather than mere intuition. My candid weakness: I commit to exciting opportunities before fully considering whether I can follow through.
What I learned The personality assessment proved more illuminating than I anticipated. I assumed it would simply corroborate what I already knew, and some of it did, but seeing my impulsiveness and follow-through deficit articulated so plainly made it difficult to dismiss. Sequencing the Career Goal Essay first was a deliberate choice by the professor — it compelled me to commit to a direction in writing before the rest of the semester pulled my attention elsewhere.
Week 2

Marketing Myself

Purpose: Translate the internal self-knowledge from Week 1 into something external — a refined LinkedIn presence, a dream job analysis, and a professional association aligned with my field.
Dream Job Analysis — Business Systems Engineer "This job is the bridge between what the business wants and what the tech team actually builds, which a degree in management information systems is a perfect foundation [for]." I also identified the genuine skill gaps: I can compose an academic paper, but I have never drafted a requirements document an engineering team would actually implement. That gap is now a deliberate priority rather than an invisible one.
LinkedIn & Professional Association I revised my LinkedIn headline to lead with "Systems Solution Analyst | MIS Candidate, FIU" rather than a bare job title, and listed the certifications I'm actively pursuing (CompTIA A+, ITIL 4 Foundation) as in-progress credentials instead of waiting until completion. I also researched the Association of Information Technology Professionals (AITP) as a trade organization worth joining post-graduation, given its focus on the intersection of IT and business that I'm aiming to occupy.
What I learned The Dream Job assignment was the most clarifying exercise of the semester. Reviewing actual job postings rather than speculating rendered the path forward concrete: scripting proficiency, foundational cloud knowledge, and a genuine requirements document I can reference, not merely coursework on a transcript.
Week 3

Learning From Leaders

Purpose: Examine how accomplished leaders operate, distinguish coaching from mentoring, and clarify the actual function of professional networking — then identify a practitioner in the field and engage them directly.
Executive Profile & Interview See the complete writeup in the Project Assignments section below — I spoke with someone in a systems/IT-adjacent role about their career trajectory, what they wish they'd known earlier, and how they navigate the dimensions of the job that extend beyond technical execution.
What I learned The distinction between coaching and mentoring resonated with me more than expected. I had been using the terms interchangeably my entire life. Understanding the difference reshaped how I think about whom I approach for guidance and what I should reasonably expect from that relationship.
Week 4

Getting Organized

Purpose: Construct two practical frameworks — SMART goals and the Eisenhower Matrix — to convert abstract career intentions into commitments with concrete deadlines.
DO NOW Mock interview preparation, impending work project deadline
SCHEDULE Coursework for other classes, mid-semester progress review
DELEGATE Routine reporting, coordinating interview logistics
DROP Low-priority meetings, revising work that is already sufficient
SMART Goal — Certification "I will study for and pass either the CompTIA A+ or ITIL 4 Foundation exam to add a recognized certification to my resume before the end of the year." A target date established, obstacles enumerated, and resources for support identified. Not merely an aspiration.
What I learned The Eisenhower Matrix exposed something I'm reluctant to admit: much of what feels urgent day to day is simply conspicuous, not consequential. Mapping my own obligations onto the four quadrants made it evident that I had been according low-stakes meetings the same weight as genuine deadlines.
Week 5

Getting Hired

Purpose: The nearest equivalent to a dress rehearsal for the job search — a partner-conducted mock interview, paired with genuine salary and compensation research so negotiation isn't approached blindly.
Salary Research — Systems Engineer (O*NET / Salary.com) Entry-level compensation nationally averages around $65,334 annually, though Miami runs slightly lower (approximately $61,819-$63,636 across Miami, Hialeah, and Fort Lauderdale) owing to cost-of-living differences. Competencies such as team leadership and troubleshooting can each contribute meaningful percentage increases to pay. The role is also designated "Bright Outlook," with over 31,000 projected openings annually through 2034.
What I learned Knowing the actual range before any future negotiation eliminates much of the guesswork and anxiety. I also hadn't anticipated how dramatically location affects compensation — the identical title in San Jose pays roughly $20,000 more annually than it does a short distance from where I live.
Project Assignments

Practicing the genuine pursuit

The two assignments designed to feel less like coursework and more like the actual job search.

Week 3

Executive Profile & Interview

For this assignment, I contacted a professional in a systems/IT-adjacent field and interviewed them regarding their career trajectory, leadership philosophy, and counsel for someone beginning their career. The most significant insight I gained: technical proficiency secured the initial opportunity, but interpersonal capability is what sustained their advancement. That observation corroborated everything I had already discerned about myself through the personality assessment.

I genuinely enjoyed this assignment. Most of my coursework is solitary, so the opportunity to engage directly with an established professional — rather than simply reading about the field — was the most invigorating exercise of the semester for someone with my disposition.

Week 5

Partner Mock Interview

My partner conducted an authentic interview, including the following questions:

  • Q1Tell me about yourself.
  • Q3Tell me about your strengths.
  • Q4What are your areas of opportunity?
  • Q5Where do you see yourself in five years?
  • Q10Do you have any questions for me?

Overall, it proceeded well — the strengths question came easily, since I had recently dissected my own personality report in considerable depth, which gave me specific, substantiated answers rather than generic ones. The "areas of opportunity" question proved more difficult to answer candidly aloud, even though I had already documented my follow-through deficit in writing. There is a marked difference between privately acknowledging something and articulating it to another person directly. That was the genuine takeaway: rehearsing the uncomfortable answers matters as much as rehearsing the straightforward ones.

A podcast episode was also referenced as part of this course's project work. That component isn't ready to share yet — it will be incorporated here once complete.
Something Extra

Extending beyond the syllabus

Free-Write

Why "Systems Solution Analyst" is the appropriate title for someone like me

Every assignment in this course returned, from a different vantage point, to the same underlying premise: understand yourself, then pursue the role that genuinely suits that understanding. The personality report confirmed that I am inclined toward people and momentum, not toward solitary engagement with a specification sheet all day. Yet the Dream Job and Salary research assignments confirmed that technical competency isn't optional either — the most lucrative, future-proof iterations of "bridge between business and technology" roles all presuppose genuine scripting, cloud, and systems literacy underneath the interpersonal skills.

So the additional initiative I undertook outside of class: I began a small personal project that mirrors the actual workflow of a Business Systems Engineer, the dream role I researched in Week 2. I'm drafting simple requirements documents for minor process inefficiencies I observe at work, then actually constructing a script or workflow remedy for them, rather than merely describing the fix on paper. It echoes the same counsel I offered myself in that assignment — don't simply assert that you can perform the job; construct something that demonstrates it. Progress is gradual, but it marks the first instance where coursework and my actual career direction have aligned closely enough that I wanted to continue the effort after the assignment had already been submitted.
